A Replication of Cheng & Hoekstra (2013)

Does Strengthening Self-Defense Law Deter Crime or Escalate Violence?

by Michael Valentino Ochoa

Castle Doctrine Statutes

A long-standing principle of English common law is that an individual has a “duty to retreat” before using lethal force against an assailant. The exception is when the assault occurs at your home (your castle).

In 2005, a slew of U.S. states (starting with Florida) expanded the Castle Doctrine to apply anywhere a person has a legal right to be, not just at home. These new laws granted immunity from civil liability for the use of justified defensive force. It also shifted the burden of proof to the prosecutor, who had to show that an individual acted unreasonably.

Castle Doctrine and Crime

The main reason for "stand your ground" laws was to provide additional legal leeway to potential victims in a self-defense situation. Yet one can expect these expanded versions of the castle doctrine law alters incentives. Under these expansions, the cost of using lethal force is curbed as there is a lower probability that one will be found guilty of the death/injury inflicted. Thus, victims are more likely to respond to threats by using lethal force.

Whether or not people respond to these changes is an empirical question. It is important to estimate the costs and benefits of these self-defense laws. To assess these possibilities, this replication exercise evaluates the impact of the expanded castle doctrine law on homicide, motor vehicle theft, larceny, robbery, and aggregated assault.

Identification

Cheng & Hoekstra’s (2013) research design is a difference-in-differences (DiD). It exploits the staggered adoption of castle doctrine laws between 2000 and 2010. The sample comprises 50 U.S. states (21 eventually treated) over 11 years. The estimation method has two-way fixed effects, where different types of crimes are considered as outcomes, CDL is the treatment variable that equals the proportion of year t in which state i has an effective CDL, Xit is a vector of time-varying control variables, λt is year fixed effects, μi is state fixed effects, τt is region-by-year fixed effects, and εit is the error term.

The identifying assumption is that, in the absence of CDL, adopting states (treated group) would have experienced changes in crime similar to those of nonadopting states (control group) in the same region of the country between 2000 and 2010. To assess whether the parallel trends assumption is reasonable, I look for visual evidence.

Altogether, this plot shows why Cheng & Hoekstra (2013) included region-by-year FE as Georgia should be compared with other southern states in the same year, rather than with a national control group.

Data Prep for Multistate Homicide Regression

To ensure our results are more accurate, I added further complexity to the model by adding population weights to account for state-level population differences and including fixed effects.

Why State Fixed Effects?

State fixed effects (FE) control for unobserved heterogeneity that is constant across observations belonging to a state, such as demographics (long-term racial composition, median age, urbanization levels), geography (presence of a major cities, crime affected by seasonal climate), police infrastructure (officers per capita, policing style, judicial strictness, sentencing norms), baseline crime levels, cultural norms around self-defense, long-standing gun ownership rates and enduring economic differences. These FE are baked into each state and don’t fluctuate year to year. State FE removes them as sources of bias, ensuring comparisons are within-state over time rather than across states with different baselines. In other words, state FE captures how crime changed in a state after CDL was enacted in 2005, relative to the state's crime trend prior to its passage.

Why Year Fixed Effects?

Year FE control for unobserved heterogeneity that is constant across observations that belong to all states, such as national business cycles that affect crime rates, federal sentencing revisions, and federal funding changes for law enforcement, nationwide shifts in gun culture, and nationwide declines in crime. These factors are common to all states; therefore, I sweep them aside. Year FE absorbs these national shocks, so anything affecting all states each year is not mistaken for treatment effects. This is essential because crime rates oscillated in the 2000s for reasons unrelated to CDL.

Why Region-by-Year FE Fixed Effects?

Region-by-Year FE control for unobserved heterogeneity that is constant within each region-year, such as region-specific business cycles, regional policing cultures, regional political climates, demographic transitions, and regional crime trends. Region-by-year FE ensures that treated states are compared only to other states in the same region and year. It removes the region-specific time shocks, allowing each region to have its own baseline crime rates, distinct from those of other regions, for every year. This is critical as CDL was adopted in staggered waves across regions. Altogether, region-by-year FE enables the Midwest, West, South, and Northeast to exhibit their own distinct trends each year. It also captures regional time-varying confounders (i.e. region-level variation that could dilute CDL adoption) that may mimic treatment effects, thereby undermining the credibility of the identification.

Falsification Tests

Before running these regressions, I assumed that CDL would only affect a state’s homicide rates or crimes linked to physically harming others. Whereas I would expect crimes such as motor vehicle theft and larceny to remain constant during the passing and implementation of the expanded laws. To test this assumption, I ran two falsification tests.

Deterrence Effects

Beyond testing if homicide rates change, we would like to understand if CDLs have a deterrent effect. That is, if potential criminals know their victims can legally shoot them in self-defense, does that drive a drop in robbery and aggravated assault?
